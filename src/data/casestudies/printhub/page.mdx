  title="Reduced Task Completion Time by 30% on Printhub by revamping user flow"
  image1="/casestudies/printhub/1.1.webp"
  role="Product Designer"
  team="3 Developers"
  duration="5-6 weeks"
  overview="PrintHub makes document printing simple, but the experience still required users to repeatedly verify their settings before placing an order. Because printing is irreversible and mistakes cost both time and money, users lacked confidence and spent unnecessary time double-checking every step.

This redesign focused on reducing that uncertainty by improving visual feedback, clarifying primary actions, and rebuilding the interface using Material 3. The result was a faster, more predictable printing experience that reduced unnecessary interactions and helped users complete printing tasks 30% faster."

        <BodyText>Printhub wasn’t like your average ecommerce, people used it when they needed it. For a user it was meant to exist for exactly the time it requires them to print and then leave them later. Current experience was far from ideal, causing user to second guess their own choices and not surfacing information causing heavy cognitive load that made them reverify their own choices.</BodyText>

<Section id="decisions">
  <SubSection>
    <ContentSection 
      sectionLabel="How I revamped the card to fix reverification problem" 
      heading="Building Card to Glance" 
      >
          <BodyText>When redesigning the card, my goal was to help users quickly understand which print setting had been changed and which were still at their default values. I believed users kept rechecking settings because they had to remember their previous actions, leading to unnecessary verification.</BodyText>
          <BodyText>Following the recognition over recall principle, I explored ways to make applied settings visible at a glance. Since <span>visual cues are faster to process than text, I began by exploring a visual-first approach using icons.</span></BodyText>
    </ContentSection>

    <ImageSection
      src="/casestudies/printhub/4.1.webp"

    <ContentSection>
        <BodyText>This is when I came across Gmail. It's card pattern solved two problems at once. Its leading icon naturally handled selection, removing the need for a separate checkbox, and its familiar interaction made it easier to gain stakeholder confidence than introducing a new pattern.</BodyText>
        <BodyText>After team discussions, we replaced the existing interaction with a Gmail-inspired card pattern.</BodyText>
    </ContentSection>
